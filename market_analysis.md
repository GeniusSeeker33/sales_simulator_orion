## Section 4 — Firearms-Industry Market Fit

### Who Actually Buys This

**The most likely buyer at a firearms distributor:**
- **Title:** VP of Sales, Director of Sales, or National Sales Director
- **Team:** Reports to CEO or COO; owns sales headcount, quota attainment, and rep onboarding speed
- **Budget:** Training/enablement typically $500–$2,000 per rep per year at mid-size distributors; enterprise tools can reach $5,000/rep/year. A team of 40 reps at $30/month = $14,400/year — well within discretionary budget for a VP.
- **Decision dynamic:** VP of Sales owns the decision. HR/L&D is an influencer, not a buyer. CFO approves if over ~$25k/year.

**Secondary buyer at a manufacturer:**
- **Title:** National Sales Manager, Regional Training Manager
- **Dynamic:** Manufacturers often have smaller direct sales forces but run extensive dealer training programs. The use case shifts from internal rep training to dealer/retailer training — a different product problem.

### What Makes Firearms Distribution Sales Different

This is important to be honest about because the product currently addresses none of it:

1. **ATF compliance.** Reps cannot make certain representations about products. They must understand prohibited person rules, the "straw purchase" conversation, and what they can legally say when a dealer asks about inventory that's legally ambiguous in certain states. A rep who says the wrong thing on a recorded call creates legal exposure for the company.

2. **FFL relationships.** Every dealer is a Federal Firearms Licensee. Maintaining that relationship has compliance dimensions: dealers can lose their license, and a distributor whose rep gave bad advice can be implicated. Reps need to know how to handle a dealer who is at risk.

3. **State-level complexity.** CA, NY, NJ, MA, CT, IL, and other states have restrictions that change what can be shipped. A rep who takes an order for a product that can't be shipped to that state creates returns, compliance headaches, and damaged relationships.

4. **Product technical depth.** Firearms have specifications that matter: caliber, action type, capacity, barrel length, NFA classification. A rep who doesn't know this is useless to a dealer who's trying to stock for their customer base.

5. **MAP pricing enforcement.** Minimum Advertised Price violations are a significant issue. Reps need to know how to handle a dealer who is advertising below MAP.

6. **NICS and background check logistics.** Dealers ask about volume and timing of transfers. Reps need to know the answers without giving legal advice.

7. **The "allocation" conversation.** During high-demand periods (political events, civil unrest), certain SKUs go on allocation. Managing those conversations without damaging relationships is a distinct skill.

### Does the Current Product Handle Any of This?

No. Not one item on that list is addressed anywhere in the codebase. The customer personas are generic sales archetypes (Skeptical Buyer, Price Shopper, etc.) that could apply to any B2B product. The scoring rubric evaluates discovery, order building, objection handling, and closing — the same four dimensions used in SaaS sales training. There is no firearms-specific persona, no regulatory compliance scenario, no FFL-specific language.

To be completely honest: **the current product is a generic AI sales training tool with "Orion Wholesale" in the title.** The firearms industry positioning is a go-to-market decision, not a product reality.

### Total Addressable Market — Firearms Vertical Specifically

Conservative estimates:

- Major distributors (15–20 companies): avg 50 reps each = ~900 reps
- Mid-size distributors (20–30 companies): avg 20 reps each = ~500 reps
- Major manufacturers with direct sales (15–20 companies): avg 35 reps each = ~600 reps
- Specialty/regional (30–40 companies): avg 10 reps each = ~350 reps

**Total addressable reps in U.S. firearms distribution and manufacturing: ~2,300–2,500**

At $30/user/month: **$828k–$900k ARR** (100% penetration, which never happens)
Realistic 20% penetration: **~$166k–$180k ARR**
Realistic 10% penetration: **~$83k–$90k ARR**

**Honest conclusion: The firearms vertical alone is not a venture-scale market.** It's a viable niche business — potentially $200k–$400k ARR at healthy penetration — but not a platform company. This product's commercial viability depends on expanding to adjacent verticals.

### Top 3 Licensing Targets

**1. Medical devices — highest priority**

The sales motion is almost identical: a rep calls on a healthcare dealer or facility, needs to understand product specs deeply, must navigate compliance language (can't make efficacy claims without clinical backing), manages a portfolio of SKUs, and is judged on account coverage and repeat orders. The TAM is 10x firearms. Companies like Medtronic, Stryker, Zimmer Biomet, and Boston Scientific each have 500–2,000 sales reps and multi-million dollar training budgets. The main product changes needed: replace firearms personas with hospital/ASC buyer personas, replace ATF compliance with FDA/510(k) language rules, replace FFL dynamics with GPO/IDN contracting dynamics.

Product changes required: New persona library, new compliance module, different scoring weights (clinical evidence handling becomes a dimension), different comp plan templates.

**2. Alcohol/spirits distribution — second priority**

The structural fit is almost exact: a distributor's rep covers a territory of retail accounts (bars, liquor stores, restaurants), needs deep product knowledge (brand stories, tasting notes, production methods), manages state-specific compliance (dry counties, bottle size restrictions, ABV limits), handles allocation conversations during releases, and manages relationship dynamics with independent vs. chain accounts. Buyers: Southern Glazer's Wine & Spirits (~20,000 employees), Republic National (RNDC), Breakthru Beverage Group, Charmer Industries. Combined, these four companies have more sales reps than the entire firearms distribution industry.

Product changes required: Swap product catalog to spirits/wine, build out three-tier compliance scenarios, new personas (bar manager, restaurant buyer, chain buyer vs. independent), different leaderboard metrics.

**3. Automotive aftermarket parts distribution — third priority**

LKQ Corporation, Genuine Parts Company (NAPA network), and O'Reilly Auto Parts Pro each have large outside sales forces calling on independent repair shops. The rep's job is: know the catalog, build the relationship, manage inventory programs, handle price objections from shops comparing to online competitors. This is less regulated but the volume and scale is enormous. Genuine Parts alone has 5,000+ sales reps.

Product changes required: Catalog integration with parts lookup, new personas (shop owner, fleet manager), lower compliance complexity.

### Recommended Licensing Structure

For all three targets, the appropriate structure depends on who the licensee is:

**For a large distributor (Southern Glazer's, LKQ) buying for internal use:**
- Platform fee + per-seat: $5,000–$10,000/month platform fee + $20–30/seat/month
- White-labeled with their branding
- Custom scenario library built as part of onboarding (one-time services fee: $15,000–$30,000)
- Annual contract with 90-day notice for renewal

**For a technology partner or training company reselling to multiple clients:**
- Revenue share: 25–35% of each contract they sign
- Co-branded (their logo + your technology)
- You maintain the AI infrastructure; they own customer relationships
- Minimum annual commitment to get into the program

**For a manufacturer licensing to distributors (e.g., SIG Sauer licensing to their distribution network):**
- Flat license fee: $50,000–$150,000/year per vertical market
- Includes custom scenario library for their products
- They can sublicense to their distributor network
- This is the highest-leverage model if you can land it

### Three Defensible Price Points

**Floor: $15/seat/month**

What justifies it: The product has a working AI roleplay loop, real comp plan data, and gamification. It delivers value if used. At $15, for a team of 40 reps, that's $600/month — a rounding error in a sales budget. This is the price to get Orion as a paying pilot customer given the current state (no backend, no real product catalog, broken API calls, no independent validation).

**Target: $35/seat/month**

What justifies it: This requires fixing the broken API calls, deploying a real database (Supabase or PlanetScale would cost $25/month to run), adding real product catalog data, and showing that reps who use the tool close deals faster. At this price you're competitive with Second Nature and Hyperbound and you're asking the buyer to make a real bet on you. Do not charge this until you have one paying customer who has seen a result.

**Ceiling: $75/seat/month**

What justifies it: This requires the ATF/FFL compliance module, validated scoring calibration, a call recording integration (so the sim closes the loop on real calls), and 2–3 reference customers with measurable ramp improvement. At $75 with 40 reps, that's $3,000/month — a real line item that needs a real business case. This is an 18–24 month target, not a today price.

### Is $30/User/Month Right?

**Honest answer: It's overpriced for what the product delivers today, and underpriced for what it should deliver.**

Today the product has a broken AI core, no backend, and no firearms-specific content. Charging $30 before fixing those things and showing value to one customer is setting yourself up for churn after month two. The CFO will ask what they got for $14,400/year and the answer will be thin.

The right sequencing: fix the product → pilot at $0 or nominal cost with Orion → collect evidence of rep improvement → charge $30+ at the next company based on that evidence.
